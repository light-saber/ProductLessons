## ðŸ’¡ Concept: Data-Informed > Data-Driven

Shreyas Doshi draws a sharp line between being **data-driven** and **data-informed**. Data-driven means you let metrics dictate decisions. Data-informed means you *use* data as one powerful input alongside judgment, customer intuition, and strategic context.

Why does this matter at your level? Because the most consequential PM decisions â€” entering a new market, killing a beloved feature, reorganizing a platform â€” rarely have clean data to back them up *before* you make the call. The data only confirms (or refutes) *after*.

**Example:** When Brian Chesky decided Airbnb should pivot from "growth at all costs" to curated, quality-controlled stays, the existing metrics screamed against it. Listings growth, GMV, host acquisition â€” all pointed toward continuing the playbook. But qualitative signal (trust erosion, guest complaints, regulatory pressure) told a different story. The data was *right* about the past. His judgment was right about the future.

**The trap at big companies like Walmart:** In large orgs, data becomes a *political shield*. People cherry-pick metrics to justify pre-decided conclusions. "The data says..." becomes a way to avoid accountability for a judgment call. Senior PMs who always need a dashboard before moving are actually *under-performing* â€” they're outsourcing their thinking to a spreadsheet.

**Today's exercise:** Pick one decision you're currently deliberating on. Write down:

1. What the data says (metrics, A/B tests, user analytics)
2. What the data *can't* tell you (strategic shifts, competitive moves, customer emotions, 2-year trajectory)
3. What your gut says â€” and *why* (trace it to experience, pattern-matching, or customer conversations)

If columns 2 and 3 are empty, you're being data-driven. If all three are filled, you're data-informed. A Principal PM's superpower is articulating the *reasoning behind the judgment*, not just the numbers.
